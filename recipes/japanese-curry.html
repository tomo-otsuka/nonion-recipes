Allium-Free Japanese Curry

Traditionally, Japanese curry relies heavily on deeply caramelized onions to provide its signature sweetness and savory backbone. This recipe achieves that exact same comforting, rich, and complex flavor profile using a clever combination of Hondashi, grated apple, and a touch of tomato paste.

Yield 4 servings
Prep Time 15 mins
Cook Time 30 mins
💡
Flavor Swap: The Umami Backbone

To replicate the depth of caramelized onions, we build an aromatic base using grated apple for natural sweetness, tomato paste for savory depth, and Hondashi (dashi powder) to provide an intense umami punch. A tiny pinch of asafoetida (hing) bloomed in butter mimics the subtle pungency of alliums.

Ingredients

The Custom Roux

  • 3 tbsp unsalted butter
  • Pinch (1/8th tsp or less) hing (asafoetida) — to bloom in the melting butter
  • 3 tbsp all-purpose flour
  • 1 tbsp pure curry powder (ensure it is an allium-free blend, like S&B Oriental)
  • 1 tsp pure garam masala (no fillers or alliums)

The Meat & Produce

  • 1 lb boneless skinless chicken thighs (or beef chuck), cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 1 tbsp neutral oil (like canola or avocado)
  • 1 large carrot, cut into rolling wedges (rangiri)
  • 1/2 carrot, peeled and finely grated
  • 1 large Yukon gold potato, peeled and cut into chunks
  • 1 medium Fuji apple, peeled and grated
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, finely grated

The Umami & Flavor Base

  • 2 cups water
  • 1 tsp Hondashi powder
  • 1/2 tsp MSG (monosodium glutamate)
  • 1 tbsp tomato paste
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tsp honey
  • Salt and black pepper to taste

For Serving

  • Cooked short-grain Japanese rice

Instructions

  1. Prep Your Ingredients: Grate the ginger, the apple, and half of a carrot. Keep them together. Chop the remaining whole carrot into wedges (rangiri style) and the potato into bite-sized chunks. Cut your chicken or beef into bite-sized pieces.
  2. Bloom the Hing & Make the Roux: Melt the 3 tbsp of butter in a small saucepan over medium-low heat. Once melted and bubbling slightly, drop in the pinch of hing. Let it sizzle in the butter for about 10 to 15 seconds until fragrant (this mellows the raw sulfur notes). Immediately whisk in the 3 tbsp of flour. Cook this mixture, stirring constantly, for 10 to 15 minutes until it toasts to a deep, peanut-butter brown color. Remove from the heat, whisk in the 1 tbsp curry powder and 1 tsp garam masala until smooth. Set aside.
  3. Sear the Protein: Heat the 1 tbsp of neutral oil in a heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Lightly season your meat with salt and pepper, then add it to the pot. Sear until browned on all sides. Remove the meat and set it aside, leaving the remaining fat in the pot.
  4. Build the Flavor Base: Lower the heat to medium. Add your grated ginger, apple, and half-carrot to the pot. Sauté for 3 to 5 minutes until the mixture softens, darkens slightly, and begins to stick to the bottom. Stir in the 1 tbsp tomato paste and cook for exactly 2 minutes until it deepens from bright red to a dark brick color.
  5. Simmer the Curry: Return the seared meat to the pot along with the chopped potatoes and wedged carrots. Pour in the 2 cups of water, 1 tsp Hondashi, 1/2 tsp MSG, 1 tbsp soy sauce, and 1 tsp honey. Scrape the bottom of the pot to release any browned bits into the liquid. Bring everything to a boil, then reduce the heat to low, cover, and simmer for 15 to 20 minutes, or until the potatoes and carrots are fork-tender.
  6. Thicken and Finish: Turn off the heat completely. Ladle about 1/2 cup of the hot curry broth from the pot into your reserved roux. Whisk vigorously until the roux becomes a smooth, lump-free liquid. Pour this thinned roux back into the main pot and stir gently until completely incorporated. Turn the heat back to low and simmer uncovered for 5 minutes. The curry will thicken significantly and become glossy. Taste and adjust seasoning with a pinch of salt or a splash of soy sauce if needed.
  7. Serve: Serve hot over short-grain Japanese rice!
